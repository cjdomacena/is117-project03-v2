1965

Space Race

The battle between the USA and Russia was the root case of the invention of the internet. On October 4, 1957 the Russians launched the first man-made satellite in space, the Sputnik.

J.C.R. Licklider

J.C.R Licklider was an American psychologist and computer scientist In 1962, ARPA hired Licklider for IPTO (Information Processing Techniques Office). His idea was that computers will help us to easily share information through networks. The problem was that when you connect three terminals in different locations, one terminal can only talk to one terminal and not to both terminals.

Dr. Leonard Kleinrock

Dr. Leonard Kleinrock first introduced the Queueing Theory to network and communications systems. However, the process of resource sharing is still slow. To speed up the resource sharing between networks, Kleinrock implemented Demand Access (You don’t get it until you need it) and Distributed Control (No one switch or device should have the authority to control the network, instead All devices share the authority of functions of the network).

Paul Baran

Paul Baran was asked by ARPA to create a system to transfer information between computers. He introduced "Hot Potato Routing" Hot potato routing - a routing technique enabling packet routing without storing them in buffers. Instead, this technique continuously transfers data packets until they reach their destination without the packets having to wait or be stored in a buffer.

1969

ARPAnet

In 1969, two universities set up the first computer network to be the recipient of the first “node-to-node” network transmission through ARPAnet. The first computer was located in a research lab at UCLA, the second was at Stanford, and lastly was in MIT, which was the lab that is to send the messages to both universities. The computer at UCLA successfully received the first message “LOGIN”, and Stanford only received the note’s first two letters. However, this breakthrough opens up new ways of communicating through multiple computers. And by the end of 1969, all computer networks at all universities were connected. Had this failed, who knows what would have happened to the internet that we know today.

1971

Ray Tomlinson

A computer engineer Ray Tomlinson invented the first email program under ARPAnet’s network. The concept behind it is to have the ability to send simple messages to another person across the same network.

The "@" Symbol

Ray Tomlinson made an obvious decision to pick the “@” symbol since it tells the user “at” what computer they are in.

1973

Since the birth of ARPAnet, the number of computer networks have exponentially grown. Number of computer networks have emerged like LAN (Local Area Network), it links computer networks in offices or buildings, and WANs (Wide Area Network), it links computer networks across office buildings or campuses. But, each new network speaks a different language like a foreign country. To solve this problem, internet protocol is introduced to make each network interconnected.

TCP/IP Protocol

Robert Kahn, and Vinton Cerf proposed the idea of an internet protocol to help different networks to interconnect networks through what they called “Gateways” or known today as routers. TCP/IP governs communication among all computers on the network and dictates how information should be packaged and turned into packets. This allows us to easily interconnect different networks and send and receive information. After ten years of refining and haggling, TCP/IP began as the standard Internet protocol between all networks.

1992

A major milestone in technology came from the U.S government. President George H. W. Bush passed a law that takes the Web out of the government’s hands and makes it available to the public. This unleashed an unprecedented progress in the development of the internet.

The World Wide Web

Tim Burners-Lee introduced the world wine web: an internet that was not simply a way to send files from one place to another but was itself a “web” of information that anyone on the internet could retrieve. As a result, it allows us to have the internet that we know today.

1993

Mosaic

The first browser that allows the public to easily traverse the “web” of information on the internet. Mosaic allows users to have access to the internet and browse the internet by being user-friendly. From the conception of ARPAnet to the internet that we know today, it’s conception has been a huge part of our daily lives. It helped us to be interconnected to each other in the comfort in our own home. It is safe to say that the internet has been a driving force in the innovations in technology that we have today.
